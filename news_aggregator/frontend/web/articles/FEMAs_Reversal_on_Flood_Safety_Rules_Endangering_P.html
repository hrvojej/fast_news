Summary:

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) has ceased enforcement of the Federal Flood Risk Management Standard, a rule requiring public buildings damaged in flood zones to be rebuilt to withstand future floods to qualify for FEMA funding.

This decision, outlined in a February 4 memo by Adrian Sevier, FEMA's chief counsel, is argued to endanger public safety and potentially violate federal law.

The rule, initially proposed in 2016 under President Barack Obama, was revoked by President Donald Trump in 2017, reinstated by President Joseph R. Biden Jr. in 2021, and finalized by FEMA in July, and now paused again by Donald Trump.

Experts, including David A. Super, a law professor at Yale University, and Jennifer Nou, a professor at University of Chicago Law School, suggest that while agencies generally have discretion over rule enforcement, this pause could be illegal if it amounts to an abdication of responsibility.

The decision has been criticized for increasing the risk of future flood damage and contradicting efforts to reduce government waste.

Chad Berginnis, executive director of the Association of State Floodplain Managers, stated that not enforcing the rule jeopardizes public safety and constitutes wasteful spending of taxpayers' money.

Interesting Facts:
  • ●The Federal Flood Risk Management Standard was designed to protect public buildings like schools, libraries, and fire stations.
  • ○The home-building industry opposed the rule, citing increased construction costs.
  • ● President Donald Trump revoked the executive order for the flood standard twice.
  • ○The goal of the rule was to save taxpayers' money by preventing repeated rebuilding of damaged or destroyed structures in flood plains.
  • ●Deanne Criswell, the head of FEMA at the time, said that the rule was created to stop the cycle of response and recovery.
