Summary:

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) has ceased enforcement of the Federal Flood Risk Management Standard, a rule requiring public buildings damaged in flood zones to be rebuilt to withstand future floods to qualify for FEMA funding. This decision, outlined in a February 4 memo by Adrian Sevier, FEMA's chief counsel, has raised concerns about public safety and potential violations of federal law.

The rule, initially proposed in 2016 under President Barack Obama, was revoked by President Donald Trump in 2017, reinstated by President Joseph R. Biden Jr. in 2021, and finalized by FEMA last July. Donald Trump again revoked the executive order. Adrian Sevier stated the agency won't enforce it while considering amendments.

Legal experts question the legality of this pause.

David A. Super, a law professor at Yale University, argues that FEMA must follow a formal process to reverse a regulation, including public notice and comment periods. Jennifer Nou, a professor at the University of Chicago Law School, notes that while agencies have enforcement discretion, it's not unlimited. Chad Berginnis, executive director of the Association of State Floodplain Managers, stated the safety of people is being jeopardized.

Interesting Facts:
  • ● The Federal Flood Risk Management Standard was intended to reduce the cycle of repeated damage and rebuilding of public buildings in flood plains.
  • ○ The home-building industry opposed the rule, citing increased construction costs.
  • ● Deanne Criswell, former head of FEMA, emphasized the rule's goal of saving taxpayer money.
  • ○ Reversing a regulation can take months or even longer, requiring public notice and review of comments.
  • ● Chad Berginnis argued that not enforcing flood standards constitutes wasteful spending of taxpayer money.
