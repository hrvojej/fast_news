Summary:

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) has ceased enforcement of the Federal Flood Risk Management Standard, a rule requiring public buildings damaged in flood zones to be rebuilt to withstand future floods to qualify for FEMA funding.

This decision, outlined in a February 4 memo by Adrian Sevier, FEMA's chief counsel, follows Donald Trump's revocation of the executive order that established the standard, initially implemented by Barack Obama in 2016 and reinstated by Joseph R. Biden Jr. in 2021.

The rule's original intent, as stated by former FEMA head Deanne Criswell, was to protect people and property and to save taxpayers' money by preventing repeated damage and rebuilding of structures in flood plains.

However, legal experts, including David A. Super and Jennifer Nou, question the legality of FEMA's decision to stop enforcing the regulation without following proper administrative procedures.

Chad Berginnis, executive director of the Association of State Floodplain Managers, stated that pausing the rule jeopardizes public safety and contradicts efforts to reduce government waste.

Interesting Facts:
  • ● The Federal Flood Risk Management Standard was first proposed in 2016 in response to an executive order from President Barack Obama.
  • ○ The home-building industry opposed the rule, citing increased construction costs.
  • ● President Donald Trump revoked the executive order supporting the flood standard on two separate occasions.
  • ○ FEMA stated the flood rule is "under review" per the president's executive order.
  • ● Experts say that the pausing of enforcement could endanger public safety and violate federal law.
