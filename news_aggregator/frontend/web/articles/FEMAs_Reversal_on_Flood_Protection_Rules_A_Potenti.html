Summary:

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) has decided to cease enforcement of the Federal Flood Risk Management Standard, a rule requiring public buildings damaged by floods to be rebuilt to withstand future flooding to qualify for FEMA funding.

This decision, outlined in a February 4 memo by Adrian Sevier, Chief Counsel of FEMA, follows Donald Trump's revocation of the executive order that established the standard, initially created under Barack Obama and reinstated by Joseph R. Biden Jr..

The rule, first proposed in 2016, aimed to protect people and property, and to save taxpayer money by preventing repeated rebuilding of flood-damaged structures. Donald Trump initially revoked the order in 2017; Joseph R. Biden Jr. signed a new order in 2021; and a final rule was issued by FEMA in July.

Experts, including David A. Super and Jennifer Nou, suggest this pause may be illegal, as it bypasses the required legal process for reversing a regulation.

Chad Berginnis, executive director of the Association of State Floodplain Managers, stated the non-enforcement would endanger public safety and contradict efforts to reduce government waste.

Interesting Facts:
  • ● The Federal Flood Risk Management Standard was one of the Joseph R. Biden Jr. administration's most significant efforts to address the increasing costs of disasters.
  • ○ The home-building industry opposed the rule, citing increased construction costs.
  • ● Deanne Criswell, former head of FEMA, stated the rule's goal was to stop the "cycle of response and recovery, and rinse and repeat."
  • ○ Donald Trump has twice revoked the executive order for a federal flood standard.
  • ● Reversing a regulation can take months or longer, and until that happens, the existing rule remains in effect.
