Summary:

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) has ceased enforcement of the Federal Flood Risk Management Standard, a rule requiring public buildings damaged in flood zones to be rebuilt to withstand future floods to qualify for FEMA funding. This decision, outlined in a February 4 memo by Adrian Sevier, FEMA's chief counsel, is not publicly announced and potentially violates federal law.

The rule, initially proposed in 2016 under President Barack Obama, was revoked by President Donald Trump in 2017, reinstated by President Joseph R. Biden Jr. in 2021, and finalized by FEMA last July.

The stated purpose of the standard was to protect lives and property and reduce wasteful spending by preventing repeated damage to buildings in flood plains.

Deanne Criswell, former head of FEMA, emphasized the rule's role in breaking the "cycle of response and recovery."

Donald Trump again revoked the executive order supporting the standard, leading to FEMA's current pause in enforcement. Adrian Sevier's memo directs immediate implementation of this pause while FEMA considers amending the rule.

David A. Super, a law professor, argues that FEMA cannot legally stop enforcing a regulation without a formal process, including public notice and comment.

Jennifer Nou, another law professor, notes that while agencies have enforcement discretion, it's not unlimited, particularly if it amounts to abandoning responsibility. Chad Berginnis, executive director of the Association of State Floodplain Managers, criticizes the move as jeopardizing public safety and contradicting Donald Trump's goal of reducing government waste.
