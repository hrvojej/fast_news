Summary:

The Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) has ceased enforcement of the Federal Flood Risk Management Standard, a rule requiring public buildings damaged in flood zones to be rebuilt to withstand future flooding to qualify for FEMA funding. This decision, outlined in a memo by FEMA chief counsel Adrian Sevier, is a response to Donald Trump's revocation of the executive order that established the standard.

The Federal Flood Risk Management Standard, initially proposed in 2016 under President Barack Obama, aimed to mitigate the increasing costs of flood damage exacerbated by climate change. Donald Trump revoked the order in 2017, but President Joseph R. Biden Jr. reinstated it in 2021, leading to a final rule issued by FEMA in July 2023.

The rule's purpose was to protect people, and property, and to prevent the cycle of repeatedly rebuilding damaged structures in flood plains with government funds, a point emphasized by former FEMA head, Deanne Criswell.

However, with Donald Trump again revoking the executive order, Adrian Sevier's February 4 memo instructs FEMA to pause enforcement while considering amendments.

Legal experts, including David A. Super of Yale University and Jennifer Nou of the University of Chicago Law School, question the legality of this move. They argue that halting enforcement without following proper administrative procedures, including public notice and comment periods, may violate federal law.

Chad Berginnis, executive director of the Association of State Floodplain Managers, stated that not enforcing the rule endangers public safety and constitutes wasteful government spending, contradicting Donald Trump's stated goal. The home-building industry had previously opposed the rule, citing increased construction costs.

Interesting Facts:
  • ●The Federal Flood Risk Management Standard applies not only to public buildings but, in some cases, to private homes rebuilt in flood plains.
  • ○Barack Obama first initiated the effort to create a federal flood standard through an executive order in 2016.
  • ●One method of complying with the rule is elevating a structure above the expected height of a future flood.
  • ○The Association of State Floodplain Managers argues that pausing the rule constitutes wasteful spending.
  • ● Legal experts suggest that FEMA's decision to pause enforcement may be an overreach of presidential authority.
