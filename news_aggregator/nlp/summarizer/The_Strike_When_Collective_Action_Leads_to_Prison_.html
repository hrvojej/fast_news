Summary:

Inmates at Pelican Bay State Prison in California initiated a series of hunger strikes, beginning in 2011, to protest the conditions of solitary confinement and the practice of debriefing.

Prisoners faced prolonged periods of isolation, often lasting for decades, with inadequate food and limited contact with the outside world. The Mandela Rules, the U.N.'s standard for solitary confinement, sets a 15-day limit; anything beyond that is considered a form of torture.

The strike organizers, who were often identified as gang members based on race or reading materials, were required to debrief — provide information about gangs — to be released from solitary. Many found it nearly impossible to get out of solitary confinement once placed there.

A second, larger hunger strike was launched in 2013, involving nearly 29,000 inmates across California, protesting against these conditions.

This 2013 Hunger Strike lasted for two months, with 100 prisoners still refusing food at its conclusion.

The strike organizers achieved solidarity by forming a non-hostility pact, uniting rival groups within the prison system.

The collective action demonstrated the power of unity and brought attention to the issue of prolonged solitary confinement in California prisons, although systemic change has been slow.

Interesting Facts:
  • ● Inmates used a unique communication method: emptying water from their toilets and shouting through the commodes to speak with other prisoners in isolation.
  • ○ The non-hostility pact signed by strike organizers was a "show of force" that surprised the California Department of Corrections.
  • ● Authorities attempted to break the strike by offering unlimited food to any inmate who would stop participating.
  • ○ The 2013 strike involved inmates from two-thirds of California's 33 prisons, in addition to four private out-of-state prisons.
  • ● Many inmates not in conditions that were object of the original strike still joined the 2013 strike, recognizing the power of broad action.
